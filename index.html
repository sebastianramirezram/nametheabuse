Why This Matters

Harm often becomes visible only after language catches up.

Many people first encounter abuse as confusion, self-doubt, guilt, or a steady feeling that something is wrong but hard to explain. Naming a pattern can create enough distance to see it more clearly.

That is the tone of this project: serious without being punishing, honest without becoming sensational, and structured to help readers feel grounded rather than overwhelmed.

Ask Yourself

Do some behaviors get dismissed as “normal relationship tension” even though they feel shrinking, frightening, or exhausting?

Pause

Have you ever explained someone’s behavior as protective, intense, or loving when what you actually felt was constrained?

Remember

Recognition does not need to be dramatic to be real. Quiet realizations still matter.

What You Will Find Here

Clear definitions that separate care from control, conflict from coercion, and discomfort from patterned harm.

Practical language for naming red flags that are often minimized because they look ordinary at first.

Wider context on structural violence, social norms, and the systems that shape what gets excused or believed.

How To Move Through The Site

Read linearly

Start with foundations and move forward page by page for the fullest context.

Read selectively

Jump directly to red flags or resources if that is what you need right now.

Read slowly

Use the callouts as pauses rather than instructions. They are there to support reflection.

A Gentle Orientation

This platform is meant to inform without pushing, and support without assuming.

Some readers are here because a topic feels personal. Others are educators, researchers, or students trying to understand the language better. The design goal is to make room for both: enough structure to feel trustworthy, and enough softness to keep the material readable.

That is why the site uses narrower reading widths, slower pacing, and recurring guidance boxes. They are not decoration. They are part of how the content tries to remain emotionally usable.

About The Project

A small, intentionally understated note from the creator.

This project was created as a way to better understand and name patterns of harm that often go unnoticed. It is an ongoing effort to make research on abuse, gender, and power easier to approach with care and reflection.

Sebastian Ramírez is a PhD student at Northeastern University whose interests include gender, education, and climate change. You can find occasional updates on @SebastianRamRa1 and on Instagram at @nametheabuse.
